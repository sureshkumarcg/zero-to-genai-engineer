Diagnosis
Three architectural constraints. Try each of these right now on your phone.

Turn off web search in ChatGPT settings, or type "answer from your own knowledge only, do not search the web." You will see these failures instantly.

Anticipating your question: "But ChatGPT can search the web now." Yes. Web search is just RAG for the public internet. It still can't search your Confluence, your Slack, your contracts database, or anything behind your VPN. That's what we're building.

Hallucination
Ask (with search off): "Give me the exact final score and scorers from yesterday's World Cup match."
The model will name players, give you minute marks, describe a penalty shootout. It sounds like a match report. None of it happened. It's writing fan fiction, not facts.
It doesn't know it's wrong. That's the dangerous part.
Knowledge Freeze
Ask: "What was SpaceX's IPO price on June 12, 2026?"
SpaceX went public three weeks ago at a $350B valuation. The biggest IPO in history. But any model trained before June 2026 will tell you SpaceX is still a private company. Because in its training data, it is.
Three weeks is all it takes to be dangerously outdated.
Data Isolation
Ask: "What decisions were made in our team's standup meeting this Monday?"
You were there. Your team discussed a blocked deployment and a client escalation. The model will invent a plausible standup summary about "reviewing priorities and assigning action items." Sounds fine. Completely fictional.
No web search fixes this. Your internal world is invisible to every LLM.

Resolution
RAG solves this by forcing the model to generate from evidence, not memory

At inference time, you retrieve relevant documents from your own corpus and place them in the model's context window. The model reads before it writes. Every answer traces back to a source.

Framing
Think of it the way you already work

You don't write a client deliverable from memory. You pull the relevant research, cross-reference it, then synthesize. RAG is that exact workflow, automated at machine speed.

The model is a brilliant analyst who just joined your firm yesterday. Give them access to your knowledge base and they become dangerous. Without it, they're guessing.

Closed-book (LLM alone)
Writes from training memory. No verification step. Cannot distinguish what it knows from what it's invented.
Open-book (with RAG)
Retrieves relevant material first. Reads it. Then writes an answer grounded in that material, with citations.
How it works
Embeddings turn meaning into geometry

Every chunk of text gets projected into a high-dimensional space. Sentences that mean similar things end up near each other. This is what makes retrieval by meaning possible.

"Terminate the agreement" and "end the contract" land in the same region of vector space. No keyword overlap required.

Infrastructure
Vector databases make semantic search fast enough for production

You index your document embeddings once. At query time, the user's question becomes a vector, and the database returns the nearest neighbors. Sub-100ms across millions of chunks.

Traditional search
"cancel subscription" only matches documents containing those exact words. Misses "termination process" completely.
Vector search
"cancel subscription" retrieves everything semantically related: termination, cancellation, account closure, opt-out.
